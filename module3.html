3.1 – What is a scientific model?
  • 3.1.1 – examine the types of models that may be used in science, including:
    • 3.1.1.1 – diagrams
    • 3.1.1.2 – physical replicas
    • 3.1.1.3 – mathematical representations
    • 3.1.1.4 – analogies
    • 3.1.1.5 – computer simulations
3.2 – What makes scientific models useful?
  • 3.2.1 – examine the use of scientific models, including but not limited to:
    • 3.2.1.1 – epidemic models
    • 3.2.1.2 – models of the Universe
    • 3.2.1.3 – atomic models
    • 3.2.1.4 – climate models
  • 3.2.2 – outline how models have been used to illustrate, simplify and represent scientific concepts and processes
  • 3.2.3 – explain how scientific models are used to make predictions that are difficult to analyse in the real world due to time frames, size and cost
  • 3.2.4 – assess the effectiveness of models at facilitating the understanding of scientific processes, structures and mathematical relationships through the use of:
    • 3.2.4.1 – diagrams
    • 3.2.4.2 – physical replicas
    • 3.2.4.3 – mathematical representations
    • 3.2.4.4 – analogies
    • 3.2.4.5 – computer simulations
  • 3.2.5 – evaluate how scientific models draw on a growing body of data from a wide range of disciplines and technologies to refine predictions and test new hypotheses
3.3 – When should a particular model be used?
  • 3.3.1 – explain why new evidence can challenge the use of existing scientific models and may result in those models being contested and refined or replaced, including but not limited to the development of:
    • 3.3.1.1 – epidemic models
    • 3.3.1.2 – models of the Universe
    • 3.3.1.3 – atomic models
    • 3.3.1.4 – climate models
  • 3.3.2 – compare the limitations of simple and complex scientific models
3.4 – How can a model be constructed to simplify understanding of a scientific concept?
  • 3.4.1 – investigate a scientific concept or process that can be represented using a model, by:
    • 3.4.1.1 – planning a model with reference to the scientific literature
    • 3.4.1.2 – constructing a model using appropriate resources to represent the selected scientific concept
    • 3.4.1.3 – demonstrating how the model could be used to make a prediction
    • 3.4.1.4 – presenting and evaluating the model through peer feedback
