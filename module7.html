7.1 – How can a claim be tested?
  • 7.1.1 – plan and conduct an investigation based on testing a claim, and consider:
    • 7.1.1.1 – validity of the experimental design
    • 7.1.1.2 – reliability of the data obtained
    • 7.1.1.3 – accuracy of the procedure, including random and systematic error
  • 7.1.2 – using examples, evaluate the impact that sample selection and sample sizes can have on the results of an investigation
  • 7.1.3 – compare emotive advertising with evidence-based claims, including but not limited to:
    • 7.1.3.1 – health claims on food packaging
    • 7.1.3.2 – claims about the efficacy of a product
7.2 – What factors can affect the way data can be interpreted, analysed and understood?
  • 7.2.1 – using examples, justify the use of placebos, double-blind trials and control groups in order to draw valid conclusions
  • 7.2.2 – evaluate the impact of societal and economic influences on the collection and interpretation of data, including but not limited to:
    • 7.2.2.1 – predicting variations in climate
    • 7.2.2.2 – suggesting remedies for health conditions
    • 7.2.2.3 – manipulating statistical data
7.3 – What type of evidence is needed to draw valid conclusions?
  • 7.3.1 – evaluate how evidence of a correlation can be misinterpreted as causation, including but not limited to:
    • 7.3.1.1 – the Hawthorne effect
    • 7.3.1.2 – 1991 study that linked hormone replacement therapy to coronary heart disease
    • 7.3.1.3 – the Mozart Effect on child development
7.4 – How does the reporting of science influence the general public’s understanding of the subject?
  • 7.4.1 – examine a contemporary scientific debate and how it is portrayed in the mainstream media, including but not limited to:
    • 7.4.1.1 – accuracy of information
    • 7.4.1.2 – validity of data
    • 7.4.1.3 – reliability of information sources
  • 7.4.2 – evaluate the use and interpretation of the terms ‘theory’, ‘hypothesis’, ‘belief’ and ‘law’ in relation to media reporting of scientific developments
  • 7.4.3 – compare the difference in reporting between a peer-reviewed journal article and a scientific article published in popular media
  • 7.4.4 – analyse how conflicts of interest can result in scientific evidence being suppressed, misinterpreted or misrepresented and discuss measures to counteract such conflicts, including but not limited to:
    • 7.4.4.1 – tobacco industry and lung cancer
    • 7.4.4.2 – fossil fuel industry and climate change
    • 7.4.4.3 – commercial industries researching products for market
    • 7.4.4.4 – asbestos mining and lung cancer
  • 7.4.5 – describe the halo effect and, using examples, explain how the influence of positive perceptions can result in the rejection of valid alternative perspectives, including but not limited to:
    • 7.4.5.1 – celebrities endorsing products or viewpoints
    • 7.4.5.2 – popular brand companies making misleading advertising claims
  • 7.4.6 – using examples, analyse a pseudo-scientific claim and how scientific language and processes can be manipulated to sway public opinion, including but not limited to:
    • 7.4.6.1 – astrology
    • 7.4.6.2 – numerology
    • 7.4.6.3 – iridology
7.5 – Can the scientific community and process of peer review find ‘the truth’?
  • 7.5.1 – conduct an investigation using secondary sources to research a scientist who has falsified their scientific experimental results, and discuss the process used to uncover the fraudulent research
  • 7.5.2 – analyse the scientific debate surrounding ‘publication’ and discuss the implications of scientists’ need to ‘publish or perish’
  • 7.5.3 – evaluate the increasing volume of scientific papers being published and assess the feasibility of science to effectively manage, review, replicate and validate investigations, for example:
    • 7.5.3.1 – Pons and Fleischmann's cold fusion announcement in 1989
    • 7.5.3.2 – Alex Smolyanitsky’s falsified scientific paper using the pseudonyms Maggie Simpson and Edna Krabapple, accepted for publication in 2014
    • 7.5.3.3 – Tom Spears’ nonsense journal submission accepted for publication in 2013
  • 7.5.4 – analyse the benefits of peer review in relation to the advancement of science
  • 7.5.5 – discuss the impact of fake science journals on the public perception of science
