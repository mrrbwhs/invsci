Module 5: Scientific Investigations (Summary Scaffold)

5.1 – What initiates an investigation?
  • 5.1.1 – research the factors that led scientists to investigate the following, including but not limited to:
    • 5.1.1.1 – peptic ulcers (Marshall and Warren)
    • 5.1.1.2 – plant growth (Von Helmont)
    • 5.1.1.3 – microwaves (Spencer)
  • 5.1.2 – propose a reason for the scientists undertaking their investigations above by examining the type of data or information that they sought, for example:
    • 5.1.2.1 – finding relationships or patterns in identified phenomena
    • 5.1.2.2 – testing the conclusion of a previous investigation
    • 5.1.2.3 – utilising scientific knowledge and understanding to make more accurate predictions and develop new technologies
  • 5.1.3 – determine the hypotheses that were tested in each of the scientific investigations above
  • 5.1.4 – describe where deviations from the traditional and linear models of scientific methodology were necessary in order to test each hypothesis in the investigations above
5.2 – What type of methodology best suits a scientific investigation?
  • 5.2.1 – using examples, evaluate the objectives and data collected in an investigation by a recognised scientist or team of scientists, including but not limited to:
    • 5.2.1.1 – Marshall and Warren and peptic ulcers
    • 5.2.1.2 – Eratosthenes and Earth’s circumference
    • 5.2.1.3 – Doppler and the Doppler effect
    • 5.2.1.4 – Priestley’s experiments with oxygen
  • 5.2.2 – evaluate the methodology of the scientific investigations above by:
    • 5.2.2.1 – justifying the method chosen based on the subject of the investigation and the context, for example: experimental testing, fieldwork, locating and using information sources, conducting surveys and using modelling and simulations
    • 5.2.2.2 – evaluating the relevance of the investigation by considering the peer-reviewed literature in the area of study
    • 5.2.2.3 – justifying the suitability of the type of data that is to be collected
  • 5.2.3 – develop a method most appropriate to test a hypothesis following observation
  • 5.2.4 – justify the type of methodology used to test the hypothesis
5.3 – How is the integrity of a scientific investigation judged?
  • 5.3.1 – evaluate the design of the student investigation by:
  • 5.3.2 – explaining the choice of independent, dependent and controlled variables with reference to the research question
    • 5.3.2.1 – explaining the sample selection and sample sizes used for gathering data
    • 5.3.2.2 – justifying the suitability of materials used based on their relevance to the research question, availability, cost, risk and familiarity of use
    • 5.3.2.3 – assessing the ethics of conducting the investigation by considering confidentiality, humane treatment and animal welfare
    • 5.3.2.4 – predicting an achievable time frame to conduct the investigation
    • 5.3.2.5 – justifying working individually or collaboratively
  • 5.3.3 – conduct the planned investigation and collect, record and analyse primary data
  • 5.3.4 – draw a conclusion or conclusions, and suggest further investigation or research by:
    • 5.3.4.1 – analysing the results and interpreting the data
    • 5.3.4.2 – explaining the relevance of the findings of the investigation in relation to the inquiry question and hypothesis
    • 5.3.4.3 – justifying the methodology and any changes made to improve the data collected
    • 5.3.4.4 – describing potential beneficial and harmful consequences when the findings are applied to a real-world scenario
  • 5.3.5 – evaluate the validity of the investigation by determining whether the tests measured what they were intended to measure
  • 5.3.6 – evaluate the reliability of the investigation by determining:
    • 5.3.6.1 – consistency of the results obtained
    • 5.3.6.2 – measures taken to reduce error
5.4 – What is the structure of an investigative report?
  • 5.4.1 – review a published and peer-reviewed scientific report to determine the conventions of writing a report on a practical investigation
  • 5.4.2 – use a sample of a published and peer-reviewed secondary source to identify:
    • 5.4.2.1 – the purpose of the report
    • 5.4.2.2 – measures taken to reduce error
    • 5.4.2.3 – the language style used
    • 5.4.2.4 – the presentation and structure of the report
  • 5.4.3 – compare and contrast the structures and functions of a scientific investigation and its written report
  • 5.4.4 – prepare a report on the student investigation that was carried out
